Title: Searching for new multi-component alloys with desirable stacking fault energies: The compositional dependency of binary interaction terms

URL Source: https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731

Markdown Content:
High entropy alloys (HEAs), usually containing five or more elements, occupy a vast compositional space and present a considerable challenge to experimental exploration. Predictive methods are clearly highly desirable. Approaches tried to date extend from empirical equations [[1]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0001), [[2]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0002) to first-principles calculations and include CALculation of PHAse Diagrams (CALPHAD) method and machine learning [[3]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0003), [[4]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0004).

One challenge that has arisen is that the stacking fault energy (SFE), a key parameter for modulating plasticity in _fcc_ HEAs, has in many cases been predicted by DFT to be negative, in contrast to positive experimental observations [[5]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0005). This discrepancy reduces the practical relevance of DFT SFE predictions, and the machine learning models based on them [[3]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0003), [[4]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0004). The issue has motivated considerable discussion in the literature [[6]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0006) and the way forward lies in adequate incorporation of various relevant factors such as temperature, magnetic and entropic effects [[7]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0007), [[8]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0008), [[9]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0009), [[10]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0010).

In contrast to DFT, the CALPHAD method relies on thermodynamic databases that are typically calibrated using experimental data, potentially making its predictions more consistent with experimental observations. Nevertheless, a recent comprehensive CALPHAD study of SFEs in over 300 austenitic steels [[11]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0011) reported root mean square errors (RMSEs) of 77 and 133 mJ/m 2 for the TCFE9 and TCHEA3 databases, respectively. Such large errors severely limit the usefulness of these predictions for alloy design requiring a target SFE value to be met [[5]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0005), [[12]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0012), [[13]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0013), [[14]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0014), [[15]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0015).

In this work, we address the issue of CALPHAD accuracy by comparing predicted SFEs for Co-Cr-Fe-Mn-Ni HEAs with reference values obtained via transmission electron microscopy (TEM) measurements and DFT calculations. Our results reveal that even well-calibrated CALPHAD databases can yield erroneous SFE predictions due to the compositional sensitivity of thermodynamic parameters. To address this, we propose a dynamic refinement approach to improve database accuracy and enhance the reliability of CALPHAD-based predictions.

CALPHAD computation of SFE is typically in combination with the Olson-Cohen model [[19]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0019). According to this model, the intrinsic SFE γ I S F can be expressed as the nucleation energy for a 2-layer _hcp_ (ε) embryo in a bulk _fcc_ (_γ_) matrix:(1)γ I S F=2 ρ A Δ G=8 3 a 2 Δ G,where _ρ A_ is the density of atoms in the (111) plane, _a_ is the lattice constant of _fcc_, and Δ _G_ is the energy change per atom associated with the transformation from bulk _fcc_ to bulk _hcp_. In [Eq. (1)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0001) we neglected the strain energy and interface energy associated with the phase transformation. This simplification effectively reduces the Olson-Cohen model to a nearest-neighbor approximation of the axial Ising model, considering only the bulk _fcc_ and _hcp_ phases. Although strain and interface energies are generally small compared to Δ _G_, they may become significant when γ I S F (or equivalently, Δ _G_) approaches zero. We emphasize that this simplification does not affect the conclusions of our work for two reasons. First, the majority of γ I S F values considered in this study are substantially different from zero. Second, both strain and interface energies are positive quantities, introducing a systematic error that shifts γ I S F values in the positive direction. While the exact magnitude of this shift may depend on composition, its consistent sign ensures that our comparative conclusions remain valid.

For a multicomponent alloy, the free energy _G ϕ_ of phase _ϕ_ can be written a(2)G ϕ=∑x i G 0,i ϕ+k B T∑x i ln x i+H m i x ϕ,where _x i_ is the atomic fraction of element _i_, G 0,i ϕ is the reference free energy of _i, k B_ is the Boltzmann’s constant, _T_ is the temperature, and H m i x ϕ is the enthalpy of mixing. Within the CALPHAD framework, H m i x ϕ is commonly written as [[20]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0020)(3)H m i x ϕ=∑i<j x i x j(0 L i,j ϕ+1 L i,j ϕ x i−x j C),where _C_ is 1 based on the Muggianu approximation or x i+x j in the Kohler approximation, and parameters 0 _L_ and 1 _L_ are the Redlich-Kister coefficients [[21]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0021) expressing the enthalpy of mixing of a binary alloy(4)H m i x ϕ(x i)=x i(1−x i)(0 L i,j ϕ+1 L i,j ϕ(2 x i−1)).The Kohler and Muggianu approximations give similar results for concentrated solutions, although the former performs better in dilute limits [[20]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0020). Based on [Eqs. (2)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0002)–[(4)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0004), one can rewrite [Eq. (1)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0001), using the Kohler approximation, as(5)γ I S F=8 3 a 2(∑i<j x i x j(Δ 0 L i,j+Δ 1 L i,j x i−x j x i+x j)+∑i x i Δ G 0,i).

We begin by computing _G_ 0 values for the pure elements and the binary enthalpies of mixing, shown in [Fig. 1](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0001)(a-c), using Thermo-Calc with TCHEA5 at 300 K. The corresponding Δ _G_ 0 and fitted _L_ parameters, listed in [Table 1](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#tbl0001)(a-b), are used in [Eq. (5)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0005) for SFE prediction. During this process, the lattice constant _a_ was fixed at 0.355 nm in view of its small fluctuation between 0.35 and 0.36 nm [[18]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0018). We also examined available experimental data by compiling SFE measurements from the literature for Co-Cr-Fe-Mn-Ni alloys and their sub-alloys ([Table 1](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#tbl0001)(c)), primarily obtained via TEM analysis of dislocation spacing or node morphology [[7]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0007), [[16]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0016), [[17]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0017), [[18]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0018), [[22]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0022), [[23]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0023), [[24]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0024). [Fig. 1](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0001)(d) shows the comparison between experimental SFEs and the CALPHAD predictions. The predictions based on TCHEA5 result in an RMSE of 24.2 mJ/m 2 relative to the experimental data. Notably, the predicted SFEs for the two Co-Cr-Ni ternaries are negative, in stark contrast to experimental observations. On the other hand, using back-fitted Δ _L_ parameters (with _G_ 0 fixed to TCHEA5 values) reduces the RMSE to 5.4 mJ/m 2 and rectifies the predicted Co-Cr-Ni SFEs. [Fig. 1](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0001)(e) compares the performance of TCHEA5 and fitted Δ _L_ as a function of alloy composition. It is evident that the TCHEA5-based Δ _L_ parameters incorrectly capture the Mn dependence of SFE. These findings highlight the fact that, although TCHEA5 database is well-established, it fails to accurately predict the SFEs, underscoring the dependence of Δ _L_ parameters on local compositions.

To validate the above findings, we extended our analysis to DFT computations. While numerous DFT studies on HEAs exist, the lack of standardized computational parameters hinders reliable data compilation. Hence we selected a comprehensive SFE database for Co-Ni-Cr-Fe-Mn-V-Al system, recently developed by Khan _et al_ through spin-polarized DFT computations [[3]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0003). The mean-field theory approach employed in Khan’s work circumvented variations in computed SFEs that arise from the local atomic distribution of elements in three-dimensional (3D) space [[25]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0025), [[26]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0026). The database comprises 426 alloys, with their elemental distributions shown in [Fig. 2](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0002)(a). Instead of using the TCHEA5 database, we first fitted global Δ _L_ parameters to the entire dataset to ensure proper calibration. The resulting RMSE was 20.8 mJ/m 2, comparable to the 22.6 mJ/m 2 reported by Khan _et al_ using Gaussian process regression.

Next, we assessed the performance of these global Δ _L_ parameters on specific local compositional subsets. In this context, the terms ‘global’ and ‘local’ refer specifically to the compositional space of the alloy. For an _n_-element system, this space is an (n−1)-dimensional hyperplane defined by (_x_ 1, _x_ 2,..., x n−1), where _x i_ is the atomic fraction of the _i_ th element. To this end, we selected four groups of alloys rich in three major elements: Co+Cr+Fe > 0.8, Co+Cr+Ni > 0.9, Co+Fe+Ni > 0.9, and Cr+Fe+ Ni > 0.85, containing 37, 36, 29, and 51 alloys, respectively. The DFT SFEs of these groups are shown in [Fig. 2](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0002)(b), with compositions projected to ternary diagrams. For each group, we then fitted local Δ _L_ parameters using only the SFEs within that subset and compared the CALPHAD-predicted SFEs obtained using global versus local Δ _L_ values, as shown in [Fig. 2](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0002)(c). The local parameters significantly improved predictive accuracy, reducing RMSEs from approximately 13-50 mJ/m 2 (global) to 4-19 mJ/m 2 (local). As shown in [Fig. 2](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0002)(d), some Δ _L_ parameters are highly sensitive to local composition. For example, the Δ 0 _L_ value for Cr-Fe based on the Cr-Fe-Ni-rich subset is 80 kJ/mol higher than that derived from the Co-Cr-Fe-rich subset.

While the superior performance of local Δ _L_ parameters over global ones may initially appear to be a mere consequence of better fitting, there is, in fact, a thermodynamic justification for this behavior. To illustrate this more rigorously, we draw inspiration from the definition of chemical potential. In thermodynamics, the chemical potential of element _i_ in a multicomponent system is defined as [[27]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0027)(6)μ i=∂H′∂n i|S′,P,n k,;k≠i,where _S_′ and _P_ are the entropy and pressure, respectively, and _n k_ denotes the mole numbers of all components except _i_. The prime indicates an extensive thermodynamic property. This expression shows that _μ i_ inherently depends on the alloy composition through inter-element interactions.

Analogously, we define a compositional potential that captures the variation in enthalpy when the relative proportions of two elements change while keeping their combined fraction constant:(7)μ i,j=∂H∂x i|S,P,x k,;k≠i,j=−∂H∂x j|S,P,x k,;k≠i,j.Like _μ i_, this potential _μ_ _i,j_ is also composition-dependent, as demonstrated for the Cr-Fe pair in [Fig. 3](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0003). When combined with geometric thermodynamic models such as the Muggianu and Kohler approximations used in this work [[20]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0020), [Eq. (7)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0007) explicitly shows that the effective Redlich-Kister interaction coefficients vary with composition. This implies that compositional effects influence binary interaction terms in any CALPHAD model, even beyond the specific approximations employed here. Thus, the advantage of local parameterization is not just numerical-it reflects intrinsic thermodynamic variation with alloy composition.

Given that Δ _L_ captures the influence of local alloy composition, globally fitting it across a wide compositional space may be both unnecessary and inefficient. While global fitting can yield a reasonable average estimate, a more effective strategy is to start with an initial set of Δ _L_ parameters and iteratively refine them to optimize specific material properties, such as the SFE, within a targeted compositional range. This approach is illustrated in [Fig. 4](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0004), where we first established reference SFEs for Cr-Fe-Ni alloys and then used CALPHAD to identify a target composition starting from an initial guess, subject to practical constraints.

The reference SFEs were computed using the axial Ising model (AIM) [[28]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0028), [[29]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0029) as(8)γ I S F=5 E A B A B C−5 E A B C,where the subscripts refer to stacking sequences. This formulation simplifies the data processing and achieves higher-order accuracy in capturing layer interactions compared to the conventional method involving the _ABAC_ stacking [[30]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0030). For ternary systems, both _ABABC_ and _ABC_ structures were modeled using orthorhombic supercells with randomly distributed atoms. The _ABABC_ supercell had dimensions of approximately 7.5 × 5.2 × 6.1 nm 3 and contained 21,600 atoms; the _ABC_ supercell was 7.5 × 5.2 × 3.7 nm 3 with 12,960 atoms. Energy minimization was carried out with atoms fixed and the cell relaxed isotropically. For statistical robustness, ten atomic configurations were evaluated per structure. These parameters are justified by the work of Arora et al., who found that SFE variations up to 200 mJ/m 2 are negligible in sufficiently large simulation cells [[26]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0026). Nevertheless, we note that local SFE fluctuations, arising from diverse nearest-neighbor environments, can effectively pin dislocations and thereby alter deformation mechanisms [[6]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0006), [[31]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0031). All simulations were performed using LAMMPS [[32]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0032) with a modified embedded atom method (MEAM) potential developed for Co-Ni-Cr-Fe-Mn systems [[33]](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#bib0033).

The objective was to identify a composition with reduced Ni content, desirable from a cost perspective, while keeping the SFE within a target window of 25 ± 2.5 mJ/m 2. We began with composition _C_ 0=Cr 0.25 Fe 0.1 Ni 0.65 and derived the initial Δ _L_ parameters by fitting the SFEs of _C_ 0 and five nearby compositions. Since a ternary system requires six Δ _L_ parameters, fitting at least six distinct compositions is necessary, according to [Eq. (5)](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#eq0005), assuming known values of _a_ and Δ _G_ 0. Alternatively, the initial Δ _L_ parameters can be sourced from prior estimations or thermodynamic models.

Once the initial parameters were determined, we iteratively searched for an optimal composition using the following steps:

*   1.
_Sampling_: A set of candidate compositions was randomly selected from the surface of a hypersphere (radius=0.1) centered at the current composition, with constraints: (1) no increase in Ni content, and (2) a minimum distance of 0.025 from prior candidates. A relatively large number of candidates was used, leveraging the low computational cost of the next step.

*   2.
_Screening_: SFEs of the sampled compositions were computed using CALPHAD with the current Δ _L_ values. The target composition, _C_ t, was defined as the one whose SFE lies within 25 ± 2.5 mJ/m 2 and is closest to 25 mJ/m 2.

*   3.
_Model Update_: The reference SFE of _C_ t, denoted γ t r, was evaluated using the AIM model. To simplify, we used the nearest composition from the reference dataset in [Fig. 4](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0004)(a) with a known SFE. The Δ _L_ parameters were then updated by minimizing the Euclidean distance from the previous values, subject to the constraint |γ t u−γ t r|<t, where γ t u is the SFE predicted by the updated Δ _L_, and _t_ was set to 2 mJ/m 2.

These steps were repeated iteratively, with each newly identified composition connected by an arrow in [Fig. 4](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0004)(b). Compared to the full reference map in [Fig. 4](https://linkinghub.elsevier.com/retrieve/pii/S1359646225005731#fig0004)(a), this directed optimization effectively traces the 25 mJ/m 2 contour within only a few iterations.

In summary, this work tackles the challenge of designing complex alloys with targeted SFEs, a key property influencing mechanical performance. We show that accurate SFE prediction requires model parameters that reflect local chemical environments. By comparing model predictions to both experimental and first-principles benchmarks, we find that composition-specific refinement significantly improves predictive reliability over generic, globally fitted models.

Building on this insight, we developed a dynamic refinement strategy that iteratively updates model parameters using minimal initial data. This approach enables efficient navigation of composition space to identify candidate alloys with tailored properties. As a case study, we applied it to the Cr-Ni-Fe subsystem and successfully identified low-Ni alloys that retain desirable SFEs, highlighting a promising direction for developing more sustainable and cost-effective alloy systems.

Overall, this study emphasizes the power of adaptive, data-guided modeling in accelerating alloy design, especially for multicomponent systems where traditional trial-and-error approaches are infeasible.
